What do these numbers mean? (click to expand)

Sharpe Ratio

Measures how much return you earn per unit of total risk taken. Risk here means volatility (how much the daily return bounces around). Calculated annualized.
  • Below 1 mediocre — the risk you took barely produced extra return
  • 1–2 solid
  • 2–3 very good — institutional-grade
  • Above 3 elite — rare territory
Alpha compound 5.38 means: for every 1 "unit" of volatility Alpha took, it produced 5.38 units of annualized excess return. Very high because the 30% fee structure + allocation sizing + high win rate all amplify the return-per-bounce.

Sortino Ratio

Same idea as Sharpe, but only penalizes downside volatility (losing days). The argument: you don't care if your account jumps around when it's going up — you only care when it's dropping. Sortino is almost always higher than Sharpe for skewed strategies (few big wins, many small losses).
  • Above 2 preferred
  • Above 3 elite downside management
Alpha compound 5.32 means: measured against only the losing-day volatility, Alpha returns 5.32 units per unit of downside risk. The fact that Sortino (5.32) is close to Sharpe (5.38) tells you the volatility is fairly symmetric — losses aren't notably fatter than wins.

"Compound" vs "Additive" — why two numbers?

Compound uses daily AccR% as the return series. Daily AccR is a % of current equity, so as equity grows the same AccR% represents bigger dollar moves. This slightly inflates the Sharpe on long winning streaks.

Additive uses raw per-trade Net R values directly. Each trade's R is measured against the trade's own stop, not current equity. This is the "cleaner" academic approach — the one hedge funds use.
Alpha: compound 5.38 vs additive 4.48 — the compound figure is inflated by about 20% due to the equity-growth effect. Omega shows a bigger gap (4.86 vs 2.83) because its returns are more variable. When in doubt, trust the additive number. Compound is kept for continuity with how AccR is displayed elsewhere.

Ulcer Index

Measures how deep and how long drawdowns last — combined into one number. A system with a single −50% DD that recovers in 5 days has a low Ulcer. A system that grinds at −30% for 100 days has a high Ulcer even though the depth is smaller.
  • Think of it as "pain duration × pain depth"
  • Lower is better
  • Under 10 = smooth · 10–20 = normal · Over 25 = rough ride
Alpha 24.3, Omega 15.6, Blend 15.5 — Alpha is the rougher of the two because it holds overnight, exposing it to gap risk. The blend's Ulcer matches Omega's even though Alpha alone is worse — that's a diversification win.

Max Drawdown

The worst single peak-to-trough decline in compound equity. Doesn't tell you how long it lasted (see "Time underwater" panel below for that).

How to read this chart (click to expand)

What the lines show

  • Solid gold/cyan line = the average Net R per trade across the most recent 25 trades. It moves as every new trade is added and the oldest rolls off. Think of it as a 25-trade-wide moving average of how well the system is performing right now.
  • Dotted line (same color) = the full-history average Net R. This is the system's long-run "baseline" — where the rolling line SHOULD hover around if the system is behaving normally.
  • Dashed line = mean minus 2 standard deviations. This is the alert threshold. Statistically, the rolling average should only drop below this line about 2.5% of the time by random chance alone.

What to do when the line drops below the dashed band

The system is performing worse than its historical norm by a margin that's unlikely to be pure luck. It doesn't mean the system is broken — drawdowns happen. But it's a signal to investigate rather than just trade through it. Possible causes: the setup conditions are no longer present in the market, market regime has shifted, or you've been unlucky in a stretch that will mean-revert.
Why 25 trades? Short enough to react to real degradation but long enough to smooth out single-trade noise. Traders like David Bean, Kevin Davey, and Bob Pardo all recommend this window size for live system health monitoring.
Max DD tells you damage that already happened. This chart warns you BEFORE the damage becomes catastrophic.

What is an "episode" and what do these durations mean? (click to expand)

What counts as a drawdown "episode"

Every time the equity curve drops below its prior all-time peak and then recovers back to that peak, that's one episode. If the curve drops, bounces a bit, drops further, and then finally recovers to peak, that's still one episode (not two). A new episode only begins after a full recovery to a new high.
Alpha shows 37 episodes. That means across 470 trading days, Alpha fell off a peak and then climbed back 37 different times. Most were small and quick; a few were deep.

What each duration actually measures

  • Median recovery — half the episodes took longer than this, half took less. Alpha's 4 days means the "typical" drawdown is resolved within a trading week. This is the number that tells you what day-to-day pain feels like.
  • Max recovery — the single longest episode. Alpha's 82 trading days (≈16 weeks) is the worst it ever took to get back to a peak. This is the tail-risk number that tells you what a bad patch looks like.
  • Duration shown in the top-5 table — for each episode, this is the total trading days from peak to final recovery (peak-to-trough + trough-to-recovery, combined).
  • Unresolved — episodes where equity is still below a prior peak at the end of the data. If unresolved = 0, every drawdown has fully recovered.

Why episodes > max DD alone

Max DD only tells you the depth of the worst pullback. A −77% drawdown that took 10 trading days to recover is violent but survivable. A −40% drawdown that grinds for 150 trading days is psychologically worse because you're stuck doubting the system every day for 7 months. The episode view shows both dimensions — depth and duration — together.
Reading Alpha vs Omega: Alpha's max recovery of 82 days is long because it's swing-based (holds overnight, gap risk). Omega's max is only 34 days — much faster because it's intraday and flattens every day. This is a real structural difference between the two systems.
